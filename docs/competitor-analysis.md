# Family Book Competitor Analysis

Pricing checked against public product pages on March 15, 2026. App store pricing and promotional offers can vary by region and over time.

## Positioning Summary

Family Book sits in a gap none of these products fully cover: a private, self-hosted family graph plus living archive, with ingestion from the tools people already use, graph-aware privacy, memorials, and multi-channel delivery to less technical relatives.

The nearest substitutes each cover only one slice:

- Genealogy products are strong at records, hints, and citations, but weak at everyday family life.
- Family photo apps are strong at low-friction sharing and grandparent engagement, but weak at sovereignty, structure, and multigenerational family context.
- Self-hosted photo tools are strong at backup and search, but weak at relationships, governance, and memorial meaning.
- Monica proves private relationship software has demand, but it is single-user PRM, not a shared family institution.

## Product-by-Product Analysis

### MyHeritage

- Name: MyHeritage
- URL: https://www.myheritage.com/
- Category: Commercial genealogy, records, DNA, and family-tree platform
- Pricing: Freemium. Free basic family site; paid annual plans include Premium, PremiumPlus, Data, Complete, and Omni. Public MyHeritage partner offers showed Complete at $299/year list, often discounted to $199/year.
- Key differentiator: Smart Matches, Record Matches, massive historical-record corpus, and consumer-friendly AI photo tools in one subscription stack.
- What they do well we should consider: Strong hinting and consistency checking; one-click chart and family-book generation; polished record-driven onboarding; useful photo restoration tools for old family media.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is optimized for research, subscriptions, and upsell, not a sovereign living-family archive. The product center of gravity is records, DNA, and discovery, not private family coordination, memorials, or low-friction everyday sharing.
- Open source?: No.

### Ancestry

- Name: Ancestry
- URL: https://www.ancestry.com/
- Category: Commercial genealogy records, family-tree, and DNA platform
- Pricing: Paid subscriptions. U.S. Discovery starts at $24.99/month, World Explorer at $39.99/month, and All Access at $59.99/month. Current list pricing also shows 6-month and annual plans.
- Key differentiator: The deepest paid records marketplace plus the most powerful commercial hint engine and DNA network effect.
- What they do well we should consider: Excellent onboarding from a blank tree; aggressive but effective hinting; source-linked timelines; clear product packaging for different user intents; robust search over huge archives.
- What they get wrong we avoid: Too subscription-heavy, too research-first, and too tied to external records and DNA monetization. It is not designed as an intimate private family operating system for living relatives, media, memorials, and notifications.
- Open source?: No.

### FamilySearch

- Name: FamilySearch
- URL: https://www.familysearch.org/
- Category: Free collaborative family-tree and family-history platform
- Pricing: Free. FamilySearch states there is only one kind of account: a free one.
- Key differentiator: A completely free, globally shared family-history platform with records, tree collaboration, and memories.
- What they do well we should consider: Best-in-class generosity; broad international and multigenerational scope; support for photos, stories, documents, and audio; serious commitment to free access; living-person privacy split.
- What they get wrong we avoid: The shared-tree model is fundamentally different from a sovereign family archive. It can feel like editing a public commons rather than tending your own family space, and its privacy model is much coarser than the graph-distance model in the spec.
- Open source?: No.

### Gramps

- Name: Gramps
- URL: https://gramps-project.org/wiki/index.php/Main_page
- Category: Open-source desktop genealogy software
- Pricing: Free.
- Key differentiator: Local-first, research-grade genealogy desktop software with deep structure, reports, and add-ons.
- What they do well we should consider: Strong data model discipline; provenance mindset; add-on ecosystem; export/report depth; no vendor lock-in; serious respect for user data ownership.
- What they get wrong we avoid: Desktop-first, single-user, and operationally heavy for normal relatives. It is excellent for genealogy work but poor as a casual family hub, grandparent surface, or living archive with low-friction sharing.
- Open source?: Yes.

### webtrees

- Name: webtrees
- URL: https://www.webtrees.net/
- Category: Open-source self-hosted web genealogy application
- Pricing: Free; self-hosted.
- Key differentiator: Collaborative genealogy on your own server with flexible privacy controls and strong GEDCOM compatibility.
- What they do well we should consider: Self-hosted web delivery; multi-user collaboration; privacy controls; broad internationalization; standards compatibility; practical publish-on-the-web model.
- What they get wrong we avoid: The UX is genealogy-admin heavy and emotionally thin. It is built to publish and manage family-history data, not to feel like a private family living room with feed, push, memorials, and passive ingestion.
- Open source?: Yes.

### FamilyAlbum (Mitene)

- Name: FamilyAlbum
- URL: https://family-album.com/
- Category: Private family photo and video sharing app
- Pricing: Free tier; Premium at $5.99/month or $59/year; Premium Pro at $10.99/month or $109/year.
- Key differentiator: Extremely polished, invite-only photo sharing for parents and grandparents, with delightful auto-generated memory products.
- What they do well we should consider: Very low upload friction; strong grandparent-friendly UX; per-photo visibility groups; background uploads; automatic movies and recap artifacts; integrated prints and photobooks.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is child-centric and album-centric rather than family-graph-centric. The product assumes a narrow parent-to-extended-family sharing pattern, not a durable multigenerational institution with sovereignty, memorials, governance, and imports from other channels.
- Open source?: No.

### Tinybeans

- Name: Tinybeans
- URL: https://tinybeans.com/
- Category: Private family album, milestone journal, and parent-sharing app
- Pricing: Free with in-app purchases. App Store pricing currently shows Tinybeans Plus at $7.99/month or $74.99/year, with legacy plans also present in some regions.
- Key differentiator: Parent-focused milestone journaling plus email recap distribution to faraway family members.
- What they do well we should consider: Milestone tracking; recap emails for grandparents; smart albums; calendar view; journal prompts; private invite-only sharing; simple engagement loop for relatives.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is optimized for parents documenting children, not for full family topology. It still behaves like a centralized consumer app, with broader app-store-style data collection and subscription mechanics than Family Book should tolerate.
- Open source?: No.

### Storyworth

- Name: Storyworth
- URL: https://storyworth.com/
- Category: Guided storytelling and keepsake book service
- Pricing: Storyworth Memoirs is $99/year and includes one hardcover book. Storyworth Celebrations is free to start, with books starting at $79.
- Key differentiator: Prompt-driven storytelling that reliably ends in a physical keepsake book.
- What they do well we should consider: Structured prompts create actual narrative output; the book is a powerful emotional artifact; the product understands that memory capture needs ceremony, not just storage.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is a project, not a family platform. There is no real family graph, no ongoing archive behavior, no sovereignty posture, and little support for day-to-day family life beyond the book workflow.
- Open source?: No.

### FOREVER.com

- Name: FOREVER.com
- URL: https://www.forever.com/
- Category: Permanent cloud memory storage, sharing, and legacy-planning platform
- Pricing: 2GB free trial. One-time storage pricing starts at $179.99 for 10GB and scales up to $10,999.99 for 2TB; add-ons such as video streaming and facial recognition are sold separately.
- Key differentiator: "Permanent" memory storage backed by succession planning and a long-term guarantee model.
- What they do well we should consider: Succession and account-after-death framing; explicit control over who inherits access; strong permanence messaging; facial recognition as a premium organization tool.
- What they get wrong we avoid: The economics are expensive and proprietary. The permanence promise depends on a company and funding mechanism rather than open formats, self-hosting, and user-controlled exports. It is storage-first, not family-graph-first.
- Open source?: No.

### PhotoPrism

- Name: PhotoPrism
- URL: https://www.photoprism.app/
- Category: Self-hosted photo management and browsing platform
- Pricing: Community edition is free and open source. Paid self-hosted memberships start at EUR2/month for Essentials and from EUR6/month for Plus; managed hosting is also available through partners.
- Key differentiator: A polished self-hosted photo library with strong search, maps, and ownership-friendly deployment.
- What they do well we should consider: Excellent media ingestion and browsing; map and metadata handling; good self-hosting story; practical upgrade path from free to supported; careful attention to photo quality and searchability.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is still a photo system, not a family institution. Relationship semantics, governance, memorial behavior, and cross-channel family participation are all outside its core model.
- Open source?: Yes for the Community edition; paid commercial memberships exist for added capabilities and support.

### Immich

- Name: Immich
- URL: https://immich.app/
- Category: Self-hosted photo and video backup platform
- Pricing: Free self-hosted software with no paywalled features. Optional lifetime support licenses are $24.99 per individual or $99.99 per server.
- Key differentiator: The closest self-hosted replacement for Google Photos, especially for mobile auto-backup and shared libraries.
- What they do well we should consider: Excellent native-mobile feel; camera-roll backup; shared albums/libraries; strong day-to-day photo retrieval; local-first privacy posture; face-based organization and modern search.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is still centered on photos first and family meaning second. The family graph, memorial system, approvals, invitations tied to real relatives, and push-to-grandparents model are not part of the product.
- Open source?: Yes.

### Monica CRM

- Name: Monica CRM
- URL: https://www.monicahq.com/
- Category: Open-source personal CRM / relationship management
- Pricing: Self-hosted is free. Hosted Monica is $9/month or $90/year, with a limited free hosted plan also available.
- Key differentiator: Private, deliberate relationship maintenance for real humans instead of social-media performance.
- What they do well we should consider: Strong reminder model; notes and journaling around people; gifts/tasks/activities; explicit anti-social-network stance; useful proof that "private relationship software" resonates.
- What they get wrong we avoid: It is basically one person's private relationship notebook, not a shared family archive. There is no multilateral family graph, shared feed, media-first experience, or memorial/public-memory layer.
- Open source?: Yes.

## Missing Features We Should Consider

These show up in at least three competitors and are not clearly present in the current spec.

- Native mobile auto-backup from camera roll, not just share-sheet posting. FamilyAlbum, Tinybeans, FOREVER, Immich, and PhotoPrism all treat background or near-background mobile ingestion as core. Family Book currently has PWA share target and import pipelines, but not "install app, everything just starts arriving."
- Real media search across captions, comments, albums, and possibly OCR/face/location signals. FamilyAlbum, Tinybeans, PhotoPrism, Immich, Ancestry, and MyHeritage all invest heavily in search or retrieval. Our spec says "Family Book becomes the best place to find anything," but the concrete search scope today is mostly people and feed filters.
- Optional local face recognition / person clustering for old-photo recovery. Immich, PhotoPrism, FOREVER, and MyHeritage all use smarter photo organization to reduce manual tagging. If Family Book is going to ingest years of WhatsApp and scanned family history, this could save huge admin effort if kept strictly local and opt-in.
- Print-grade export paths for photobooks, memorial books, or yearly family yearbooks. FamilyAlbum, Tinybeans, Storyworth, MyHeritage, and FOREVER all understand that families want physical artifacts, not just cloud storage. A lightweight export pipeline could fit Family Book well without turning it into a commerce platform.

## Features We're Right to Skip

- DNA testing, ethnicity reports, and relative-matching commerce. That is core to Ancestry and MyHeritage, but it adds privacy, regulation, and product-scope burden that has nothing to do with the spec's core value.
- Public or semi-public family trees optimized for discoverability. FamilySearch, Ancestry, and many webtrees deployments lean toward public genealogy publishing. Family Book is right to stay invite-only and child-protective.
- Full genealogy-research workflow complexity: source citations everywhere, proof arguments, record marketplace integration, and researcher-first UX. Gramps, webtrees, FamilySearch, Ancestry, and MyHeritage need that depth. Family Book should stay focused on living family context plus lightweight import and provenance.
- Gimmicky AI memory manipulation. MyHeritage's photo animation and similar novelty features may convert well, but they cut against the spec's tone of reverence, privacy, and permanence.
- Ad-tech, engagement optimization, and family-as-audience mechanics. Consumer family-sharing apps often drift toward recap loops, subscriptions, data collection, and retention tricks. Family Book is right to remain transparent, predictable, and non-algorithmic.
- Full general-purpose PRM scope. Monica usefully validates reminders and relationship notes, but Family Book should not become a catch-all CRM for friends, coworkers, debts, gifts, and non-family life admin.
